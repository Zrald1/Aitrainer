Question 1: In Newton's second law F=ma, why must you use mass in kilograms and not weight in newtons for 'm'?
Lesson: A common error in physics is mixing up mass and weight. Mass is the amount of matter (kg) and stays constant, while weight is the gravitational force (N) and depends on location. [Thinking: Students often use 'weight' casually in equations requiring mass, leading to unit mismatches.]
Answer: Because F=ma relates net force to mass, not weight; using weight in newtons would double-count gravity.

Question 2: What does the term 'impulse' mean in physics?
Lesson: In physics, 'impulse' is the product of force and the time interval over which it acts. It equals the change in momentum of an object. [Thinking: impulse links force and time to momentum change.] A longer collision time reduces the average force for the same momentum change.
Answer: Impulse is force multiplied by time, equal to the change in momentum.
----------------------------------------
Question 3: If a sealed gas container is heated, what causes the pressure to rise?
Lesson: In physics, increasing the temperature of a gas in a sealed container causes the gas particles to move faster. [Thinking: verifies cause (temperature) and effect (particle speed) relationship.] This faster motion leads to more frequent and forceful collisions with the container walls, which directly increases the pressure.
Answer: Faster-moving gas particles collide more frequently and forcefully with the container walls.
