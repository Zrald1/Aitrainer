Question 1: Why does a fingerprint at a crime scene fit the concept of reasoning in detective work?
Lesson: Reasoning involves connecting evidence to a conclusion. In a detective case, finding a suspect's fingerprint at a crime scene is direct evidence. [Thinking: The fingerprint physically links the person to the location, supporting a logical inference of presence.] This fits reasoning because you use the print to deduce they were there.
Answer: It serves as evidence to logically deduce the suspect's presence at the scene.

Question 2: What is the representativeness heuristic mistake in reasoning, and how do you avoid it?
Lesson: A common reasoning mistake is the 'representativeness heuristic,' where we judge likelihood based on stereotypes. For example, assuming someone quiet must be a librarian ignores the far larger number of salespeople. [Thinking: This illustrates over-reliance on a trait matching a prototype.] To avoid it, consciously consider base rates—the actual frequency of categories in the population.
Answer: It's judging probability by stereotype fit. Avoid it by considering base rates instead of relying on how typical something seems.

Question 3: What can analogical reasoning not reliably do, even when a comparison feels highly intuitive?
Lesson: Analogical reasoning maps structure from a source domain to a target domain. It is useful for generating hypotheses, but the mapping is based on perceived similarity rather than structural proof. [Thinking: This means a compelling analogy can still be logically false.] Therefore, an analogy alone cannot guarantee a valid conclusion.
Answer: Guarantee the conclusion in the target domain is true.
